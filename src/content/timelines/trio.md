  - year: July 9, 1905
    event: Martha Boswell is born near Kansas City, the second child of AC and Meldania Foore Boswell. (A son, Clyde Jr., had been born in 1900.) Martha is a name passed down through her mother’s family and is shared by an aunt who is better known as “Mattie”. Mattie marries AC’s brother Charles and a tight family bond is formed.
  - year: December 3, 1907
    event: Constance F. Boswell comes kicking and screaming into the world. She would lose the kick at age 3 when polio left her with partially paralyzed legs. But the scream eventually mellowed into one of the biggest voices of the century.

  - year: May 20, 1911
    event: Helvetia George Boswell made her presence known to the world in Birmingham, Alabama. Where did they get that name? According to Vet (the name she was best known by) it came off a can of condensed milk. Connie’s paralysis kept Meldania too busy to nurse the newborn. So the name on the milk can became hers. Don’t ask us where the George comes from.

    event: The Boswell family leaves Birmingham and settles in New Orleans where AC becomes the agent for the Fleischmann Yeast Company.

  - year: "1916"
    event: The Boswell family moves into the house at 3937 Camp Street which the sisters will call home for the next 20 years.
  - year: October 22, 1918
    event: Just weeks after joining the Army, 18-year old Alfred Clyde Boswell Jr. is tragically stricken with influenza and dies in a medical camp on the outskirts of New Orleans. “Clydie” had played the violin and worked as an automobile mechanic before going to serve his country.
  - year: May 17, 1919
    event: the Boswell Sisters make their first public appearance together at the French Opera House in “Veronica’s Veil”. They performed as a string trio of angels.
  - year: May 6, 1922
    event: Martha and Connie win a talent contest on radio station WAAB in New Orleans. This leads to their first professional engagement – a three-day booking at the Palace Theatre where they earned $25 apiece.

  - year: March 22-25, 1925
    event: "The Boswell Sisters make their first recording on the Victor label. Of the five songs they record, two are chosen for release: Nights When I Am Lonely and Cryin’ Blues. All three sisters perform on both songs which were written by Martha. Elements that were to distinguish the “Boswell Sound” are evident on this record. Seventeen-year-old Connie sings like the last of the red hot mammas on Cryin’ while Martha plays piano and Vet joins her in an “instrumental” bridge (the instruments they are trying to imitate are a little hard to discern and border on the squeak and gargle numbers of vaudeville). The Sisters sing harmony on Nights with Vet joining Connie and Martha on the chorus. They sing “gibberish” on the chorus, a kind of code talk, that they would continue to use with fascinating results throughout their career."

    imageAlt: Boswell Sisters — March 22-25, 1925
  - year: August 30 – September 4, 1925
    event: The Boswell’s big break. An act cancels at the Orpheum Theater, New Orleans’ top vaudeville venue and the Boswell Sisters get a chance to perform. They bring down the house and close down the schools (literally – they let classes out at the school the Sisters were attending so the students could watch their show). The Sisters performed on their instruments (Martha on piano, Connie on saxophone and Vet on banjo) as well as singing. It was a turning point in their career.
  - year: October 8, 1925
    event: Despite continued success on radio and stage, Martha Boswell elopes with Jules Picard. The marriage and subsequent birth of a son in August of 1926 slows but does not stop the professional life of the sisters. Martha and Jules divorce in 1927.
  - year: November 1927
    event: The Sisters are booked in the Orpheum Theater for a return engagement. This time it attracts the attention of the Max Richards Agency who sends a letter in early 1928 inviting the Sisters to bring their act to Chicago. Connie would later recall that it was actually a form letter that was sent to all the acts that performed in the Orpheum chain, inviting them to a “try-out” theater where booking agents would size up the acts. But the girls initially thought it was an invitation to come play a major Chicago theater. Martha’s baby and Connie’s vocal chord surgery in June 1928 cause several delays.

    imageAlt: Boswell Sisters — November 1927
  - year: September 14, 1928
    event: The Boswell Sisters make their initial tryout at the Englewood Theater. Vet Boswell recalled they were so anxious that when the curtain came up they weren’t able to play their instruments or dance, but did manage to sing. Vet laughingly recounted how the stage manager came up to them after the show to ask “Don’t you girls do anything but sing?”
  - year: October 23, 1928
    event: The Sisters sign a contract with Western Vaudeville Management Company for a 5-week tour of Arkansas, Oklahoma and Texas for $50 per performance. Vet Boswell recounted their experience of playing to audiences of blanket-wrapped Indians, flagging down trains in the middle of the night and the rigors of playing one-night stands. When she learned that women were not usually sent out on this rough and tumble circuit she asked Max Richards, “Whadda we look like? Three Ducks?”
  - year: December 7, 1928
    event: During a game of hide-and-seek at an Arkansas City hotel, Connie slips out a window to what she believes to be a porch four feet below. In the dark she fails to see the porch does not come flush with the wall and plunges 18 feet to the basement. Her sisters heard the fall and had Connie rushed to the hospital. Despite a severely bruised shoulder and ankle, Connie “goes on with the show” and continues with her performance the next day. In later interviews, Connie would describe this accident as an event that took away most of the mobility she had. Martha and Vet had to carry her for the rest of their tour.
  - year: January 1929
    event: After traveling across the country with fellow vaudeville performers, the Sisters land in San Francisco. They check into the Warfield hotel where desk clerk, Harry Leedy, takes an interest in them. They get booked on the Burt Levy vaudeville circuit and play cities in the bay area.
    image: /uploads/timeline/timeline-07.jpg
    imageAlt: Boswell Sisters — January 1929
  - year: April – June, 1929
    event: The Sisters “Go Hollywood” traveling to Los Angeles with hopes of hitting it big. After months of knocking on doors, eating spaghetti three times a day and nearly giving up they are finally hired by Paramount affiliate radio KNX in June. Their broadcasts lead to jobs in the movies as “side-mikers” whose voices were used in films but faces not seen on screen.
  - year: January-February 1930
    event: Warner Brothers affiliate KFWB hire the Sisters. Connie remembered when they began working they did a show with Russ Columbo, the Valentinoesque singer and violinist. In the beginning the show also featured a pianist. But when management found out all the things the Sisters could do, Columbo and his compatriot were given their walking papers. The Boswell’s filled a full two hours with a combination of piano solos by Martha, harmony singing, specialty songs, solos by Connie and instrumentals.
  - year: April 1930
    event: The Continental Broadcast Company recorded music in the mainland to play on radio in Hawaii. The Boswell Sisters make their first recordings since the Victor sessions in New Orleans, many of which are still available. The music is recorded in 15-minute segments, accompanied by Martha on piano. The sound is best described as “early Boswell” with many of the earmarks of harmonics, scat and tempo change firmly established. Connie’s powerful solo voice is well developed but Martha still sings solos on a few numbers. Their incredible blend and hours of rehearsal is most evident on the a cappella rendition of Love Ain’t Nothin’ But the Blues.
  - year: July 30, 1930
    event: The Sisters are heard from coast-to-coast on the “California Melodies” program, broadcast on the CBS network from KHJ in Los Angeles.
  - year: October 3, 1930
    event: Okeh Records cuts My Future Just Passed, Heebie Jeebies and Gee But I’d Like to Make You Happy with the Sisters singing and Martha on piano. Their pal from San Francisco, Harry Leedy, comes for a visit and becomes their manager. He encourages them to concentrate on singing and give up the banjo and saxophone portion of the act. No recording of their instrumental performances exist. On Halloween of that year they record, Don’t Tell Him (What Happened to Me).

  - year: November 1930
    event: NBC’s Pacific Coast Network puts the Boswell’s under contract and they return to San Francisco where they broadcast from radio station KGO. They are featured on the “Camel Pleasure Hour” in several network broadcasts. During time a woman from Oakland California writes to KGO complaining that the Boswell Sisters sound like “savage chanters”. Connie and Vet later said that the letter had so upset Martha that she had started to tear it up. But Connie grabbed it from her hands and told her sisters that this was proof that what they were doing was completely different.
  - year: February 1931
    event: Harry’s sales job in New York pays off. They sign a year-long contract with NBC and move to New York where they begin performing on WJZ. They make a stop in New Orleans in February and do a special broadcast over station WSMB to all the folks back home. They are about to bust loose.
  - year: March 19, 1931
    event: "The Sisters travel to what Connie recalled as “some place in New Jersey”. But records show that it was New York City where the Boswell Sisters made their first hit record: When I Take My Sugar to Tea and Whadya Do to Me.  The title of the latter would be what songs would ask the Boswell’s for the next five years as they invent arrangements and create harmonies like no one before them. The Brunswick house orchestra backing them includes the up-and coming Dorsey Brothers. Connie told a reporter this had been the first time they’d met these guys, so they ran through their number and showed them how they wanted to record. When they were through the band gave them a standing ovation, a rare act for a group of house musicians. But they knew better than anyone that something new and wonderful was about to happen to music."

  - year: Summer 1931
    event: Although the dates conflict, sometime during the summer Martha, Connie and Vet make their film debut, cutting a Paramount short called “Close Farm-ony”. Martha accompanies their farm fun on a piano made of hay bales. Connie is unusually mobile in this movie. Although seated for much of it, she demonstrates a range of mobility by straddling then swinging her legs to the side of a hay bale and standing in a brief scene. The short debuts in January 1932.
  - year: July 8, 1931
    event: The “Boswell Sound” is set and the Sisters’ long association with Victor Young  is cemented by the time they record It’s the Girl and It’s You for Brunswick. Victor Young’s orchestra became a favorite of the Sisters. Not only did it include the Dorsey Brothers but by this time Joe Tarto had become the bass/tuba player of choice. Joe Venutti’s playful jazz violin and Eddie Lang’s swinging guitar were also requested for Boswell sessions. The Sisters made their own arrangements (but never wrote the singing parts down) and Connie would write the charts for the orchestra. Much like African American jazz musicians from New Orleans, the Boswell’s encouraged their sidemen to “jam”. Some historians credit the Boswell’s as the first to allow white musicians this kind of creative freedom.

  - year: October 10, 1931
    event: The Boswells crash Broadway in a smashing performance at the Palace. It was the climax to a run in vaudeville houses that began in August 1931. The Sisters continued working closely with Brunswick producer Jack Kapp who will supervise their recordings for the rest of their career. They cut sides with the Kapp’s stable of stars on October 25 titled Gems from George White’s Scandals featuring Bing Crosby, Frank Munn and the Mills Brothers. Connie told a reporter that most of their recordings were made between midnight and dawn. They’d finish up a show at the Paramount or the Roxie, then go to a little bar off Broadway to pick up the Dorsey’s, Bunny Berrigan, Manny Klein, Stan King and the rest of the boys. They’d try to get the boys sobered up on black coffee, then start the recording session. But sometimes the coffee didn’t do the trick and they would ruin 20-30 “waxes” before they’d get an acceptable take. But Connie remembered them as a fun bunch of great musicians who really understood what they were trying to do with the music.

  - year: February 24, 1932
    event: “Vocal gymnastics” is a term that has been used to describe the way Martha, Connie and Vet put a song together. Nowhere is this more jaw-droppingly evident than in the amazing recording of Everybody Loves My Baby. It starts out energetically to a nice fast swing clip and soon Connie is scatting through the verse. Then, out of the blue, the Sisters double-time the chorus in gibberish. The velocity of the staccato notes, the clarity of diction and the perfection of the pitch is mind boggling. But there’s more – Connie “gulls” through the bridge and the chorus repeats again. The band comes back in at a much slower speed and a delayed cymbal crash seems to say “whoa, what just happened here?” When the sisters come back in they pick up the tempo and throw out the melody. Heck, why not throw out the words! They do and scat along in perfect three part harmony interspersed with the song title when it’s convenient.  It is a perfect 10! There’s plenty of drama on the flip side, too, which features Stop the Sun, Stop the Moon (My Man’s Gone).

  - year: Summer 1932
    event: "Between radio shows, appearances at theaters and recording a series of great songs that included Connie’s solo Hummin’ to Myself  and The Night That Love Was Born (June 16), Martha, Connie and Vet on Doggone I’ve Done It, Hand Me Down My Walkin’ Cane (June 17),  and Old Yazoo, the Sisters work in three film appearances, shot in Astoria, Queens. Next they shoot a little number where their rehearsal of Lou’siana Waddle is crashed by band leader Abe Lyman who accuses them of singing off key in the Vitaphone Short “Rambling Round Radio Row #1”. They also shot a portion of their theme song Shout Sister Shout and Crazy People for Paramount Film “Big Broadcast of 1932.” The Crazy People clip is interesting thanks to a slip that Connie seems to make at the end. Using “daffy” where she uses “crazy” in the Dec. 7 1932 recording, her sisters manage to follow her until the final verse when they can’t seem to decide whether to say “go crazy over people like you” or “go crazy over things you go”. But they cover it up so well that apparently no one noticed. Connie sits at the piano with a sort of “oh heck” look on her face. Although released years later (1937) the Boswells also record Heebie Jeebies during this same set. Those fortunate enough to have seen the Boswells perform say that these two shorts are the best examples of how the Sisters appeared onstage. But it must nor have been the best of experiences. Connie, who in her later life is described as “always smiling” has a less than pleased appearance in a photo taken at the time."

  - year: October 1932
    event: Martha Connie and Vet come home to New Orleans for the first time in almost two years. They are met at the station by the Mayor’s limousine and swept away with a police escort to a special breakfast held in their honor at the Roosevelt Hotel. After being honored by the city fathers they are whisked, sirens blazing to their family home at 3937 Camp Street. Ostensibly home for a rest and prevented from performing without the permission of CBS, the city clamors to hear their hometown sweethearts perform. Harry Leedy arranges with CBS allow them to entertain at the New Orleans Athletic Club where they had made countless appearances in their youth. They played to a packed house with ringing ovations and returned to New York more inspired than ever.
  - year: Winter 1932
    event: Their radio career flourished, they began touring and cut some classic Boswellian sides with It Don’t Mean a Thing, Lou’siana Hayride and Minnie the Moocher’s Wedding Day (Nov. 22, 1932), Crazy People and Mood Indigo (Dec. 7, 1932). On Mood Indigo could be called the truly “low down” example of the Boswell Sisters lowered pitch. Connie plunks a chorus of Mornin’ Blues right in the middle of the song and it works fabulously. They appear at the Capitol Theater and other cinema houses during January 1933 performing live onstage before the main features. This leads to a tour across the Midwest in February and March where they headline on the bill ABOVE Paul Whiteman!

  - year: June 21, 1934
    event: Two very interesting songs are recorded. Don’t Let Your Love Go Wrong starts out with a rumba beat and shakes along to the verse where it changes to a foxtrot then seamlessly goes back to the maraca infused rumba . Their switch to a slow tango on the bridge is startling but flawless. Tempo changes weren’t new but the latin beat had just entered their lexicon. Why Don’t You Practice What You Preach is another song admonishing a man to be true. There is a great example of harmonic gulling as Connie and Vet back up a brief solo by Martha with vocal banjo-like sounds. The following week they go Galveston, Texas for a four-week engagement.

    event: Connie jams with the Ramblers Dance Orchestra at Casino Harmdorff in Laren, Holland and cuts I Can’t Give You Anything But Love and Life Is a Song. The Sisters will play Paris before returning to the States in September.
  - year: October 1935

    event: Connie lays down You Are My Lucky Star and I’ve Got a Feelin’ You’re Foolin’ on October 4, then joins the Sisters for the suave rendering of Top Hat and Cheek to Cheek on October 8. On October 30th Connie joins Ray Noble and his Orchestra on the Coca Cola Show.
  - year: November or December 1935
    event: Reporter Hilda Cole asks Connie why she is performing without her sisters on radio. Connie’s answer has Vet taking care of her ailing husband who may be shipped to South America when he recovers. Martha has gone home to New Orleans to consider whether to marry a man from New York. All plans are on hold for the trio until they decide what they are going to do. This is the first time since Vet’s marriage is announced September that the public hears of  major choices that may change the course of the trio’s career.
  - year: December 1935
    event: Martha becomes engaged to George Lloyd and the couple plans a New Years’ Day wedding.

  - year: January 1, 1936
    event: Martha weds George Lloyd at St. Stephen’s Church in New York City. Connie is the bridesmaid and Vet is in attendance. The couple will settle at Lloyd-Boswell farm in Peekskill, NY.
  - year: January 6, 1936
    event: Three married ladies get together to record I’m Gonna Sit Right Down and Write Myself a Letter and the playful The Music Goes Round and Around the week after Martha takes her vows. They make several performances during January for Goodrich Silvertown Time promoting their recent recordings.
  - year: February 12, 1936
    event: The Boswell Sisters make their final recording together. The Irving Berlin lyrics seem prophetic. The first number features Connie, who hereafter will be on her own, carrying the lead in Let Yourself Go. On the second number the Sisters harmonize throughout the song, a fitting way to declare their new lives with I’m Putting All My Eggs in One Basket. Vet is pregnant at the time of the recording.
